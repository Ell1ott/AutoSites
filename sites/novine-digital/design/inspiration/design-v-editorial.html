An essay — On joinery, code, and the long quiet of structure No. II  ·  iv
Section II. — An essay, in three parts, by the studio.

On Joinery
and Code.

I. The cabinet maker's question.

A friend who restores eighteenth-century cabinets once told us that he never begins by sanding. He begins by sitting with the piece for an afternoon, sometimes two, opening the doors slowly, watching the way the light falls into the carcass, listening for the small clicks of old wood remembering a winter. Only then does he take a tool to it. The tool he picks first is rarely the one we expect.1

We have come to believe that good websites are made the same way. The first instinct, in our trade, is to open a code editor and start composing screens. The better instinct is to sit with the brief — the words a client uses for their own work, the rhythm of their sentences, the photographs they keep on the wall — and wait until the structure suggests itself.

A schema is a kind of cabinet. The drawers must be sized to what will live inside them: a project, a photograph, a small caption no one will ever notice unless it's wrong.

II. Slowness as a method.

We are not nostalgic about slowness. The web is fast for excellent reasons: people are busy, attention is precious, and a page that loads quickly is a page that loads at all. What we resist is the second kind of speed — the speed of decisions made before they were thought through.2

A schema is a kind of cabinet. The drawers must be sized to what will live inside them.

A website built in three weeks, on a familiar template, is faster to deliver and slower to live with. Six months in, every edit costs an apology. Three years in, the site is a small museum of compromises. We prefer to spend the extra fortnight at the start, drawing the structure on butcher's paper, naming the fields with the client over a long lunch, and only then turning to the code.

Slowness, used well, is a form of respect for the people who will keep the work company after we're gone.

III. What endures.

The websites we are most proud of are the ones our clients have stopped noticing. They open the CMS on a Tuesday morning, post a new photograph, write three paragraphs about a kiln firing, close the laptop, and the site holds it without complaint. The work has receded into the furniture of their week.

That is the test, finally. Not the launch screenshot, not the case study, not the award. The test is whether, in a quiet year, the structure still feels like the right room. If it does, the joinery was honest. If it doesn't, no amount of finish will save it, and we should have said so at the start.3

— Written in the studio, Amsterdam, autumn.

1. The friend in question is Pieter, of Atelier Voorhuis, who has been our patient teacher in matters of patience.
2. See also: Stewart Brand, How Buildings Learn, on the layers of a structure that change at different speeds.
3. We have, on three occasions, told prospective clients that we are not the right studio for their brief. Each time, gladly.

Method — Survey, Schema, Joinery, Finish, Tend No. III  ·  v

A method, in five movements.

Each engagement passes through these five rooms. The pace of the rooms varies; the order does not.

→
i. — Two to three weeks
Survey
We sit with the brief, the work, and — if we can — the people who make it. Long lunches, archival material, a walk through the workshop. We leave with a notebook, not a wireframe.
Keywords: listening, archive, photography, vocabulary.
→
ii. — One to two weeks
Schema
The structure of the site, named field by field. Drawn on paper first, then in a flat document the client can read aloud. No code yet — only the cabinetry of what will eventually live inside.
Keywords: fields, taxonomies, naming, content models.
→
iii. — Four to six weeks
Joinery
Code, written by hand. Templates fitted to the schema, type set with care, images optimised in a quiet pipeline. We commit small, often, and read what we wrote the next morning before continuing.
Keywords: typography, performance, semantic markup.
→
iv. — Two weeks
Finish
The slow week. Spacing, colour proofs against printed swatches, link checking, accessibility audits, performance budget enforced. The polish that no one sees, which makes everything else feel inevitable.
Keywords: QA, accessibility, performance, copy edit.
v. — Ongoing, by the season
Tend
After launch, we stay. Quarterly visits, a small retainer, edits made together rather than emailed in a panic. The site grows with the studio, like a kitchen garden — pruned in winter, replanted in spring.
Keywords: retainer, audits, slow growth, kindness.
Section IV. — Disciplines, with their durations and deliverables.

The Digital Joinery Workshop.

Engagements taken four to six per year.
New work begins each quarter.
Inquiry by letter, please.
i. — Bespoke Development.

Hand-cut code.

Clean-room HTML, CSS, and just enough JavaScript. No frameworks chosen for fashion's sake. Every dependency is something we could, if we had to, write ourselves.

Typical duration 8 — 14 weeks Deliverables Bespoke front-end, build pipeline, performance budget, written documentation. Page weight Under 120 kb on first paint, as a rule.
Related: Foundry Modern, Hours & Tides, Atlas of Quiet Rooms.
ii. — Adaptive CMS.

Cabinetry for content.

Custom CMS schemas drawn around your work, not around a template. Editors should feel that the fields were named by someone who listened. Often they were.

Typical duration 6 — 10 weeks Deliverables Schema document, CMS setup, editor handbook, two training sessions. Built upon Sanity, Payload, or a flat-file system, by temperament.
Related: Press for Slow Hours, Restoration Notebooks.
iii. — Organic SEO.

Visibility, built in.

Foundational structure rather than chasing the algorithm of the season. Honest semantics, considered metadata, page weight kept low. Discoverability that ages with the work.

Typical duration Embedded in build · 2 weeks audit Deliverables Audit, schema markup, sitemap, content guidance, quarterly review. Refusals No keyword stuffing, no link farms, no programmatic pages.
Related: Maison Renouard, Werkstatt Lichthof.
