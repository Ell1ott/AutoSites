A Case Study

Foundry Modern

The Brief

Foundry Modern came to us in early spring with a quiet problem. Their visualisation work — the kind that takes weeks to render and minutes to scroll past — was being flattened by a website that loaded slowly, sorted poorly, and felt, in their own words, like a portfolio someone else had abandoned. They did not need a louder site. They needed one that finally listened to the work.

We spent the first three weeks not designing anything. We sat in their studio in Rotterdam, watched senior architects scrub through 4K stills at 30 percent zoom, and learned that the real artefact was the image — the precise grain of concrete at golden hour, the soft fall of north light through a courtyard. The site had to disappear behind that. Everything else was scaffolding.

The resolution was a custom CMS layered atop a static site generator, paired with an image pipeline that served AVIF at full bleed without ever stuttering. We rewrote the project taxonomy from the ground up, gave each work its own long page, and made the homepage refuse to compete with them. Three months in, organic traffic had doubled. More importantly, two clients said the website felt like the studio.

They asked harder questions than any agency we'd worked with, and then they answered most of them themselves. The site we have now is the first one we don't apologise for.
— Mira Holst, Principal, Foundry Modern

Philosophy

We build slowly, on purpose.

We came to this work through other crafts — bookbinding, joinery, a long apprenticeship in a print shop where nothing ever shipped late but nothing ever shipped early either. The lessons were the same. The work tells you when it is ready. You do not tell it.

A website, properly made, is a small piece of architecture. It has load-bearing walls. It has rooms you sit in and rooms you pass through. It has weather. Most of what we are paid to do is decide where to put the weight, and how to keep the rest quiet enough that someone might actually feel something when they arrive.

We take fewer projects than we could. We write more than we strictly have to. We answer the phone. None of this scales, which is part of why we do it.

If any of that resonates — if you have a project that has been waiting for the right hands rather than the fastest ones — we would like to hear from you.

Approach

Three commitments we keep, project after project.

i.

Built to last.

We write code the way a furniture maker selects timber: for grain, for weight, for how it will age. The sites we ship five years ago still load in under a second. That is the brief, every time.

ii.

Owned, not rented.

Your domain, your data, your CMS, your code. We do not lock you into platforms that we can leave on your behalf. If you stop working with us tomorrow, you leave with everything.

iii.

Quiet, not loud.

We have never made a website that needed a confetti animation. Our work is meant to recede so the work it carries can be heard. Restraint is the whole discipline.

The Studio

A small practice, by design.

We are two, occasionally three when a project asks for it. We work from a top-floor room above a bakery in the Jordaan, with one cat and a great deal of north light. We answer email between projects, not during them.

Most of our work arrives by introduction. We are happy to talk to anyone, but we take on roughly six engagements a year. If we are a fit, we will know inside a fortnight.
